Some of the creators of this website are women and have experienced healthcare inequalities themselves. However, a majority of the creators are men. Misdiagnosis

In the United States 26% of diagnoses were misdiagnoses with a majority of that being women. Misdiagnosis and delayed diagnosis are growing problems in terms of healthcare for women. Correctly identifying an illness is key and will lead to the proper care and treatment for that person. When illnesses are left unresolved effects like hospitalization, unneeded drug intake, and anxiety can occur. On the other hand, if a healthcare professional cannot identify an illness, regular outcomes are no treatment, delayed treatment or referrals to specialists. This leads to self-advocacy on behalf of the women. Self-advocacy is extremely important when it comes to misdiagnoses. It allows women to make informed decisions about their treatment as well as communicating effectively with their healthcare professionals. Research has proven that successful self-advocacy leads to better health outcomes and an overall better healthcare experience. Another issue is women's symptoms are often dismissed before testing causing a failure to diagnose. When their symptoms are considered, women are often falsely diagnosed with a mental illness. Once you are (mis)diagnosed with a mental illness most symptoms are referred to that illness instead of the physical one. All of this is shown throughout the photo, the doubt, denial, confusion, and how it all overwhelms you.

Closing the Gap: Addressing Gender Inequities in Healthcare

The three main topics of this video were related to the gender gap in healthcare research, gender gap in cardiac health, and the social determinants of women’s health. Typically, young, white men had only been researched on in the 20th century. But there are actually many instances where women react differently than men medically. Some examples include women being more likely to experience adverse reactions to antibiotics and women having different heart attack symptoms than men. One other major factor is how pain in women isn’t as aggressively treated as pain in men. These women are more likely to be referred to a therapist than a pain clinic. More effort must be directed into eliminating racism, discrimination, and systematic oppression in healthcare.

National Women's Health Network

Many of the disparities seen in women’s healthcare can be heavily attributed to sociopolitical sexism, influencing healthcare policies and regulations. This exact issue is what started the National Women’s Health Network (NWHN), following the 1970 Pill Hearings, a testimony exposing the harmful effects of birth control. This non-profit organization fights specifically for women’s health equity and justice, doing this “by strategically shaping policy, expanding access, and providing accurate, unbiased information” (NWHN). In the more than 45 years that the organization has been established, they have succeeded in impacting women’s health by organizing to save the FDA’s Office of Women’s Health, fought in the defense of the Affordable Care Act, and acted to prohibit the selling of toxic baby powders. Those that are interested in supporting the NWHN can do so by visiting their website (https://nwhn.org) and donating or joining their membership program.

Barriers to Women in Healthcare

Women tend to face many more barriers when it comes to healthcare. Due to the higher chance of encountering a barrier, women are faced with greater health disparities and can have severe repercussions when it comes to their well-being. Nearly 30% of women have reported to have experienced a barrier when it comes to healthcare, which is far greater than the percentage of men. Most of the time single women, women without education beyond high school, and uninsured women have much higher odds of encountering a barrier or being denied healthcare than men. Socioeconomic factors and sociodemographic variables also uniquely affect women more when it comes to healthcare. Women also experienced more barriers during the COVID-19 pandemic, as many women lost their jobs and their access to healthcare was tied to their employer. Overall, women face a much more difficult path than men when it comes to receiving adequate healthcare.
